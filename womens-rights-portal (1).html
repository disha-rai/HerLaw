Education Rights

Women's Right to Education

Every girl in India has the right to equal education. These laws and articles protect that right.

Why Education Rights Matter

Education is the foundation of all other rights. Indian law guarantees girls equal access to schools, universities, and professional institutions — and prohibits gender-based exclusion. These laws also protect against fee discrimination, sexual harassment on campus, and child marriage that interrupts schooling.

Art. 21-A
Right to Free & Compulsory Education
Constitution of India · Inserted by 86th Amendment, 2002
▾
Constitutional

⚖️ Legal Text

"The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years in such manner as the State may, by law, determine." This is enforced through the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009 (RTE Act).

💬 In Simple Words

The government must provide completely free schooling to every child — including every girl — between ages 6 and 14. No school can charge fees, turn away a child, or make a girl sit for exams before completing this basic education. Private schools must also reserve 25% of seats for economically weaker students.

🔍 How to Use This Right

  1. If a school refuses to admit a girl, file a complaint with the State Education Department.
  2. If any school charges fees for RTE-quota admissions, report to the District Education Officer.
  3. Contact the National Commission for Protection of Child Rights (NCPCR) helpline: 1800-11-1800 (toll-free).
Art. 15(3)
Special Provisions for Women in Education
Constitution of India · 1950
▾
Constitutional

⚖️ Legal Text

"Nothing in this Article shall prevent the State from making any special provision for women and children." This enables the State to create women-only colleges, girls' scholarships, and reserved seats without violating equality principles.

💬 In Simple Words

Even though the Constitution says everyone is equal, it specifically allows the government to create special benefits only for women — like ladies-only colleges, higher scholarships for girls, or reserved seats in professional courses. This is called "protective discrimination" and is perfectly legal.

POSH Campus
Protection from Sexual Harassment in Educational Institutions
UGC Regulations, 2015 + POSH Act, 2013
▾
Act2015 Regulation

⚖️ Legal Text (Key Terms)

UGC (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal of Sexual Harassment of Women Employees and Students in Higher Educational Institutions) Regulations, 2015 — mandates an Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) in every university and college. "Sexual harassment" includes unwelcome physical, verbal, or non-verbal conduct of a sexual nature.

💬 In Simple Words

Every university and college in India must have a committee to handle complaints of sexual harassment — whether made by students or staff. Harassment can be comments, staring, touching, or pressure for sexual favors. If your institution does not have this committee, they are violating UGC law.

⚠️

Penalty for Non-ComplianceThe UGC can withdraw recognition or funding from institutions that fail to establish an ICC. Perpetrators face departmental action, suspension, and criminal prosecution under IPC Section 354A.

🔍 How to Use This Right

  1. File a written complaint with your college's ICC within 3 months of the incident.
  2. If no ICC exists, complain directly to the UGC at [email protected] or call 011-23604446.
  3. Use the SHe-Box online portal: shebox.nic.in for complaints.
PCM Act
Prohibition of Child Marriage — Protects Girls' Education
Prohibition of Child Marriage Act, 2006 · Amended 2021
▾
Act2021 Amendment

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 3: Any marriage where the bride is under 18 or groom is under 21 is voidable at the option of the contracting party. Under the Prohibition of Child Marriage (Amendment) Bill, 2021 — pending enactment — the minimum age of marriage for women is proposed to be raised to 21.

💬 In Simple Words

Girls cannot be married before age 18. If someone forces a girl into marriage before 18, the marriage can be cancelled by a court. Child marriage interrupts education — this law directly protects a girl's right to complete schooling. A "Child Marriage Prohibition Officer" is appointed in every district to stop these marriages.

⚠️

PenaltyAnyone who arranges, solemnizes, or abets a child marriage faces up to 2 years imprisonment or a fine up to ₹1 lakh, or both.

Art. 46
State Duty to Promote Educational Interests of Weaker Sections
Constitution of India · Directive Principle · 1950
▾
Directive Principle

⚖️ Legal Text

"The State shall promote with special care the educational and economic interests of the weaker sections of the people, and, in particular, of the Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes, and shall protect them from social injustice and all forms of exploitation."

💬 In Simple Words

The government has a duty (though not immediately enforceable in court like fundamental rights) to specifically support the education of disadvantaged women — including SC/ST women, women from poor families, and rural women. This is the basis for government schemes like Beti Bachao Beti Padhao, Kasturba Gandhi Balika Vidyalaya, and girl-child scholarships.

Workplace Rights

Women's Rights at Work

From equal pay to safe conditions, these are the laws that protect every working woman in India.

Your Workplace is Protected by Law

Multiple laws — from the Constitution to specific Acts — create a framework of rights at work. You have the right to equal pay, a safe environment, maternity leave, and protection from harassment. Employers who violate these laws face serious penalties.

ERA 1976
Right to Equal Pay for Equal Work
Equal Remuneration Act, 1976
▾
Act

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 4: "No employer shall pay to any worker, employed by him in an establishment or employment, remuneration, whether payable in cash or in kind, at rates less favourable than those at which remuneration is paid by him to the workers of the opposite sex in such establishment or employment for performing the same work or work of a similar nature."

💬 In Simple Words

A woman doing the same job as a man must be paid the same salary. An employer cannot pay a female employee less just because she is a woman. This applies to wages, allowances, and other monetary benefits. Discrimination in promotions, training, or transfers on basis of gender is also prohibited.

⚠️

Penalty for EmployerFine of ₹10,000–₹20,000 or imprisonment up to 1 year, or both. Repeat offences: up to 2 years imprisonment.

🔍 How to Use This Right

  1. File a complaint with the Labour Commissioner of your state.
  2. You can also approach a Labour Court directly.
  3. Maintain pay slips and records as evidence of wage discrepancy.
POSH 2013
Protection from Sexual Harassment at Work
Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013
▾
Act

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 3: No woman shall be subjected to sexual harassment at any workplace. Sexual harassment includes: (i) physical contact and advances; (ii) demand or request for sexual favours; (iii) making sexually coloured remarks; (iv) showing pornography; (v) any other unwelcome physical, verbal or non-verbal conduct of a sexual nature. Section 4 mandates an Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) in every organization with 10 or more employees.

💬 In Simple Words

No one at your workplace — boss, colleague, client, or vendor — can sexually harass you. Harassment includes touching, comments, asking for sexual favors, or showing explicit material. Every company with 10+ employees must have a committee to handle your complaint. Even domestic workers and women in the unorganized sector are covered — they can approach the Local Complaints Committee set up in every district.

⚠️

PenaltyEmployers who fail to set up ICC: Fine up to ₹50,000. Repeat non-compliance: doubled fine + loss of business licence. Perpetrators face criminal action under BNS (Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita) Section 75 (Sexual Harassment).

🔍 How to Use This Right

  1. File written complaint with ICC within 3 months of incident (extendable by 3 more months).
  2. If no ICC: Contact your district's Local Complaints Committee (set up by DM/Collector).
  3. File online at SHe-Box portal: shebox.nic.in
  4. Call NCW helpline: 7827170170
MBA 1961
Maternity Leave & Benefits
Maternity Benefit Act, 1961 · Amended 2017
▾
Act2017 Amendment

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 5(3): Every woman employed in any establishment for a period of 80 days in the 12 months immediately preceding the date of her expected delivery shall be entitled to 26 weeks of paid maternity leave (for first two children). Section 11A (inserted in 2017): Mandatory crèche facility for establishments with 50+ employees.

💬 In Simple Words

If you have worked for the same employer for at least 80 days in the past year, you are entitled to 26 weeks (about 6 months) of fully paid leave when you have a baby — for your first two children. For the third child onwards, it's 12 weeks. Adoptive mothers and commissioning mothers (surrogacy) get 12 weeks. Your employer cannot fire you while on maternity leave, and companies with 50+ employees must have a crèche within 500m of work.

⚠️

Penalty for EmployerImprisonment up to 1 year + fine up to ₹5,000 for denying maternity benefits.

CA 2013
Right to Representation — Woman Director on Board
Companies Act, 2013 · Section 149(1)
▾
Act

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 149(1): Every listed company and every public company with paid-up share capital of ₹100 crore or more, or turnover of ₹300 crore or more, shall have at least one woman director on its board of directors.

💬 In Simple Words

Large companies — listed on stock exchanges, or with ₹100 crore+ capital — must have at least one woman on their board of directors. This is a legal requirement, not just best practice. This law ensures women have a seat at the highest decision-making tables in Indian business.

FA 1948
Safe & Healthy Working Conditions
Factories Act, 1948 · Sections 22, 27, 66
▾
Act

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 66: No woman shall be required or allowed to work in any factory except between 6 am and 7 pm (unless state government permits night shifts with adequate safety). Section 48: In every factory with 30+ women workers, a crèche must be provided. Separate washrooms and lockers are mandatory.

💬 In Simple Words

Factories must provide: separate, clean toilets for women; a crèche if there are 30+ women workers; lockers and changing rooms; and regulated working hours. If a woman is asked to work night shifts, the employer must provide safe transport and a security escort. These conditions apply to all factories across India.

Health Rights

Health & Reproductive Rights

Laws protecting a woman's bodily autonomy, medical privacy, and reproductive choices.

Your Body, Your Rights

Indian law recognizes a woman's right to make decisions about her own body and health. From abortion access to maternal healthcare, these laws protect reproductive autonomy.

MTP 1971
Right to Safe & Legal Abortion
Medical Termination of Pregnancy Act, 1971 · Amended 2021
▾
Act2021 Amendment

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 3 (as amended in 2021): A pregnancy may be terminated up to 20 weeks with the opinion of one registered medical practitioner. Up to 24 weeks is allowed for vulnerable women including rape survivors, victims of incest, differently abled women, and minors. Up to 24 weeks also for fetal abnormalities (with Medical Board opinion). The 2021 amendment expanded the upper limit from 20 to 24 weeks and introduced the concept of a Medical Board for late-term cases.

💬 In Simple Words

A woman has the legal right to end a pregnancy up to 20 weeks with just one doctor's approval. For rape survivors, victims of incest, minors, or women with disabilities, this extends to 24 weeks. The identity of the woman seeking abortion must be kept strictly confidential — revealing it is a criminal offence. This applies to all women regardless of marital status (the 2021 amendment removed the word "married woman" from the law).

⚠️

Privacy ProtectionDisclosing the identity of a woman who had an abortion is punishable with up to 1 year imprisonment, or fine, or both (Section 5A, MTP Act).

PCPNDT
Protection from Sex-Selective Abortion & Foeticide
Pre-Conception and Pre-Natal Diagnostic Techniques Act, 1994
▾
Act

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 6: No person shall conduct or cause to be conducted any pre-natal diagnostic technique including ultrasonography for the purpose of determining the sex of a foetus. Sex determination (finding out the gender of the baby before birth to then abort it if female) is strictly illegal.

💬 In Simple Words

It is completely illegal for any doctor, hospital, or lab to tell parents the gender of their unborn baby. It is also illegal to abort a pregnancy only because the baby is female. This law was enacted to stop female foeticide — the selective abortion of girl children. You will see "Sex determination not done here" signs in ultrasound centres — this is required by law.

⚠️

PenaltyFirst offence: 3 years imprisonment + ₹10,000 fine. Second offence: 5 years imprisonment + ₹50,000 fine. Doctor loses licence permanently.

Art. 21
Right to Life with Dignity — Includes Reproductive Rights
Constitution of India · Interpreted by Supreme Court
▾
Constitutional

⚖️ Legal Text

Article 21: "No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law." The Supreme Court in K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) (Privacy Judgement) held that reproductive choices form part of the right to privacy under Article 21. In X v. Principal Secretary Health, Delhi (2022) the Court held that all women — married or unmarried — have equal right to safe abortion under Articles 14 and 21.

💬 In Simple Words

The Supreme Court has interpreted the right to life broadly to include the right to bodily privacy and reproductive freedom. This means the state cannot force a woman to carry a pregnancy she does not want, and cannot interfere with her personal health decisions. Marital status does not affect abortion rights — a single, unmarried, or divorced woman has the same reproductive rights as a married woman.

Art. 47
State Duty to Raise Level of Nutrition & Public Health
Constitution of India · Directive Principle
▾
Directive Principle

⚖️ Legal Text

"The State shall regard the raising of the level of nutrition and the standard of living of its people and the improvement of public health as among its primary duties." This is the constitutional foundation for schemes like Janani Suraksha Yojana (safe motherhood) and Pradhan Mantri Matru Vandana Yojana (maternity benefits).

💬 In Simple Words

The government has a duty to ensure good health for all citizens, especially mothers. This is why government hospitals must provide free maternity care, and why Anganwadi centres offer nutrition support to pregnant women. Under Janani Suraksha Yojana (JSY), women delivering in government hospitals receive cash assistance of ₹600–₹1,400 depending on the state.

Property Rights

Property & Inheritance Rights

Women have equal rights to own, inherit, and control property under Indian law.

Financial Independence Starts with Property Rights

Indian law — through several amendments — now gives daughters, wives, and widows equal rights to property. These rights exist regardless of whether a woman is married, divorced, or widowed.

HSA 2005
Equal Inheritance Rights for Daughters
Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005
▾
Act2005 Amendment

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 6 (amended): "On and from the commencement of the Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005, in a Joint Hindu Family governed by the Mitakshara law, the daughter of a coparcener shall — (a) by birth become a coparcener in her own right in the same manner as the son." A coparcener is a person who has a legal right by birth to their ancestral property.

💬 In Simple Words

Before 2005, only sons had an automatic birth-right to ancestral (joint family) property. The 2005 amendment changed this completely — daughters now have the exact same rights as sons to inherit ancestral property. This applies even if the father died before 2005, as long as the property was not already partitioned before December 20, 2004. The Supreme Court confirmed this in Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma (2020).

🔍 How to Use This Right

  1. Approach a civil court if family members exclude you from ancestral property partition.
  2. Contact the District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) for free legal advice.
  3. File for partition suit through a lawyer — daughters cannot be denied this right.
Streedhan
Right to Streedhan — A Woman's Personal Property
Hindu Law · Confirmed by Supreme Court repeatedly
▾
Legal Principle

⚖️ Legal Text

Streedhan (literally "woman's wealth") refers to all movable and immovable property given to a woman — before, during, or after marriage — by her parents, in-laws, husband, or any relative. The Supreme Court in Pratibha Rani v. Suraj Kumar (1985) held that Streedhan is the exclusive property of the wife and the husband has no right over it. Under Section 27, Hindu Marriage Act, court can award streedhan during divorce proceedings.

💬 In Simple Words

All jewellery, gifts, money, and property a woman receives — from her parents at marriage (dowry she receives), from her in-laws, or as gifts from relatives — belongs entirely to her. Her husband and in-laws have no legal claim over it. If her husband or in-laws keep her Streedhan against her will, she can file a criminal complaint for theft (IPC Section 378) or criminal breach of trust (IPC Section 405).

⚠️

If Streedhan is WithheldFile an FIR for criminal breach of trust under Section 405/406 IPC. The husband/in-laws can be arrested and imprisoned up to 3 years.

CrPC 125
Right to Maintenance from Husband
Section 144, Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023 · Earlier Section 125 CrPC
▾
ActBNSS 2023

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 144, BNSS, 2023 (replaced Section 125 CrPC): If a husband neglects or refuses to maintain his wife who is unable to maintain herself, a Magistrate may order the husband to pay a monthly allowance (maintenance) to her. Maintenance = financial support for living expenses including food, shelter, clothing, medical needs.

💬 In Simple Words

If your husband leaves you or refuses to support you financially, you can go to a Magistrate's Court and ask for monthly maintenance money. The court will fix an amount based on the husband's income. This right applies to wives, divorced women (until remarriage), and even women in live-in relationships (as confirmed by the Supreme Court). Under the Domestic Violence Act, 2005, maintenance can also be claimed through the DV complaint process.

Safety Rights

Safety & Protection Rights

Strong laws to protect women from domestic violence, dowry, trafficking, and gender-based violence.

You Have the Right to Live Free from Fear

Multiple laws in India specifically criminalize violence against women. You always have the right to report abuse, access emergency protection, and seek legal remedies — regardless of relationship with the perpetrator.

PWDVA 2005
Protection from Domestic Violence
Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005
▾
Act

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 3 defines "domestic violence" to include: (a) physical abuse; (b) sexual abuse; (c) verbal and emotional abuse (insults, ridicule, humiliation, name-calling); (d) economic abuse (withholding money, stopping from working). Section 12: An aggrieved woman can file an application for relief with a Magistrate. Residence Order (Section 19): A woman cannot be evicted from the shared household, even if she doesn't own it.

💬 In Simple Words

Domestic violence is not just physical beating — it includes any emotional abuse, being called names, being controlled financially, or being threatened. This Act protects women in any domestic relationship — wives, live-in partners, daughters, mothers, sisters. You cannot be thrown out of your home. The court can issue a "Protection Order" that legally bars the abuser from coming near you. You are entitled to free legal aid and can approach a "Protection Officer" in your district.

🔍 How to Use This Right

  1. Call the women's helpline: 181 (24/7)
  2. Contact the Protection Officer in your district (appointed by state government)
  3. File an application directly at a Magistrate's court — no lawyer needed
  4. NGOs registered under the Act can also help you file a complaint
DPA 1961
Protection from Dowry Demands & Harassment
Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961 · BNS Section 84/85
▾
ActBNS 2023

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 3, Dowry Prohibition Act: Giving or taking dowry is punishable. Section 498-A IPC (now Section 85/86, Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023): Husband or relatives of husband who subject a woman to cruelty — including harassment for dowry — commit a cognizable, non-bailable offence. Section 304-B IPC (now Section 80, BNS): Dowry death — if a woman dies within 7 years of marriage under suspicious circumstances — carries minimum 7 years to life imprisonment.

💬 In Simple Words

Demanding, giving, or taking dowry is illegal. If your husband or in-laws harass you for money or property after marriage, they can be arrested without a warrant. If a woman dies suspiciously within 7 years of marriage, it is presumed to be a dowry death unless proven otherwise — and the in-laws can face life imprisonment. You do not need to wait to be beaten to file a complaint — harassment alone is enough.

⚠️

PenaltiesDowry demand/harassment (BNS 85): Up to 3 years imprisonment. Dowry death (BNS 80): Minimum 7 years, up to life imprisonment. Taking/giving dowry: 5 years + ₹15,000 fine (Dowry Prohibition Act).

BNS 2023
Protection from Rape & Sexual Assault
Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023 · Sections 63–70
▾
ActNew 2023 Law

⚖️ Legal Text

The BNS 2023 replaced the Indian Penal Code (IPC) on July 1, 2024. Section 63 (Rape): Sexual intercourse without consent is rape. Minimum 10 years rigorous imprisonment to life. Section 70 (Gang Rape): Minimum 20 years to life imprisonment or death. Section 66 (Rape by a person in authority): Harsher punishment for rape by police, army, jail officers, public servants. Zero FIR: Rape can be reported at any police station regardless of jurisdiction — the FIR cannot be refused.

💬 In Simple Words

Rape is punishable by a minimum of 10 years imprisonment. Gang rape: minimum 20 years. A woman can file a rape complaint at ANY police station — the police cannot say "go to that station." The survivor's identity is protected by law — revealing it is itself a crime. Medical examination of the survivor must be done within 24 hours. Rape by a husband is recognized as an offence under new provisions. A survivor can record her statement before a Magistrate under Section 183 BNSS to protect it from being challenged later.

🔍 How to Use This Right

  1. Call 112 (emergency) or go to any police station — file Zero FIR
  2. Seek immediate medical attention — preserve evidence if possible
  3. Contact Rape Crisis Cell or One Stop Centre (Sakhi Centre) in your district
  4. One Stop Centre Helpline: 181
ITP Act
Protection from Trafficking & Sexual Exploitation
Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, 1956 · Amended
▾
Act

⚖️ Legal Text

Trafficking is defined under Section 370, BNS 2023 as recruiting, transporting, harbouring or receiving a person — especially women and children — through coercion, deception, or abuse of power for exploitation (sexual, labour, organ harvesting). Minimum 7 years to life imprisonment. Ujjawala Scheme: Provides rescue, rehabilitation, and reintegration for trafficking survivors.

💬 In Simple Words

Human trafficking — forcing, tricking, or selling women into prostitution, forced labor, or domestic servitude — is a serious crime with life imprisonment. Police have power to rescue victims and prosecute traffickers. The government runs shelters (Ujjawala homes) for survivors. If you know someone being trafficked, call the Anti-Human Trafficking helpline: 1800-419-8588 (toll-free).

Political Rights

Political & Civil Rights

Women's rights to vote, represent, and participate in governance — fully guaranteed by the Constitution.

Women's Voice in Democracy

The Constitution of India has always given women equal political rights. Recent amendments have also reserved seats for women in local governance.

Art. 326
Equal Right to Vote
Constitution of India · Article 326
▾
Constitutional

⚖️ Legal Text

Article 326: The elections to the House of the People and to the Legislative Assembly of every State shall be on the basis of adult suffrage; that is to say, every person who is a citizen of India and who is not less than 18 years of age on such date as may be fixed in that behalf... shall be entitled to be registered as a voter. No discrimination based on gender.

💬 In Simple Words

Every Indian woman aged 18 or above has the fundamental right to vote. This has been so since 1950. No one — not family members, in-laws, or community leaders — can legally prevent a woman from voting. Voter ID cards can now be obtained independently; women do not need permission from husbands or fathers.

Art. 243D
One-Third Reservation in Panchayats & Municipalities
73rd & 74th Constitutional Amendment Acts, 1992
▾
Constitutional Amendment

⚖️ Legal Text

Article 243D(3): Not less than one-third of the total number of seats to be filled by direct election in every Panchayat shall be reserved for women. Article 243D(4): Not less than one-third of the total number of offices of Chairpersons in the Panchayats at each level shall be reserved for women. Several states (Bihar, Jharkhand, Rajasthan, Uttarakhand) have increased this to 50% reservation.

💬 In Simple Words

At least 1 in every 3 seats in village panchayats, panchayat samitis, and zila parishads must be filled by women. Similarly, at least 1 in 3 Sarpanch/Pradhan posts must be reserved for women. Many states have gone further, giving 50% of panchayat seats to women. The 106th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2023 (Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam) also reserves 33% of Lok Sabha and state assembly seats for women — to be operationalised after delimitation.

Art. 19
Freedom of Expression & Occupation for Women
Constitution of India · Article 19
▾
Constitutional

⚖️ Legal Text

Article 19(1): All citizens shall have the right — (a) to freedom of speech and expression; (b) to assemble peaceably; (c) to form associations; (d) to move freely throughout India; (e) to reside in any part of India; (g) to practise any profession, or to carry on any occupation, trade or business. These rights apply equally to women.

💬 In Simple Words

A woman has the full right to speak her mind, write, protest, choose any career or business, live anywhere in India, and move freely — without needing permission from any man. No husband, father, or community can legally restrict these freedoms. The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that these rights cannot be curtailed on the basis of gender or family "honour."

Protection Laws

Key Protection Laws for Women

PWDVA 2005
Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005
In force from October 26, 2006
▾
Comprehensive Act

⚖️ Key Provisions

Covers 4 types of abuse: Physical (hitting, kicking), Sexual (forced intercourse), Emotional/Verbal (insults, threats), Economic (withholding money). Provides: Protection Orders, Residence Orders, Custody Orders, Monetary Relief, Compensation Orders. Applies to women in any shared household — wives, live-in partners, mothers, daughters, sisters.

💬 In Simple Words

This is India's most comprehensive law on domestic abuse. It covers all forms of abuse — not just physical. A woman can apply to a Magistrate for immediate protection. The court can order the abuser out of the house, order payment of expenses, and award custody of children. A "Protection Officer" in every district helps women access this law for free.

🔍 Helplines

  1. Women's Helpline: 181 (24/7, free)
  2. Police: 112
  3. NCW: 7827170170
  4. One Stop Centre (Sakhi): 181
POSH 2013
Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013
In force from December 9, 2013
▾
Act

⚖️ Key Provisions

Section 2(n): "Sexual harassment" = unwelcome acts or behaviour (directly or by implication): physical contact, demand for sexual favours, sexually coloured remarks, showing pornography, any unwelcome physical/verbal/non-verbal conduct. Section 4: Every employer with 10+ employees MUST form an Internal Complaints Committee (ICC). Section 6: Unorganized sector workers use Local Complaints Committee (LCC) at district level.

💬 In Simple Words

Any sexual harassment at work is illegal and must be handled by the employer. All organisations with 10+ employees must have a complaints committee. Even home-based workers, agricultural labourers, and domestic workers are covered — they approach the district LCC. Complaints must be resolved within 60 days. Anonymous tips about non-compliance can be filed with the District Magistrate.

DPA 1961
Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961
Amended 1983, 1986
▾
Act

⚖️ Key Provisions

Section 2: "Dowry" = any property or valuable security given or agreed to be given directly or indirectly at or before or after the marriage. Section 3: Giving or taking of dowry = imprisonment up to 5 years + fine not less than ₹15,000 or value of dowry (whichever is higher). Section 7: All offences under this Act are cognizable (police can arrest without warrant).

💬 In Simple Words

Both giving and taking dowry is illegal. Cash, jewellery, vehicles, or property demanded as a condition of marriage — all fall under dowry. The police can arrest without warrant. The Dowry Prohibition Officer in each district can investigate complaints. Crucially, even if the parents of the bride voluntarily "give" dowry, both sides commit an offence.

POCSO 2012
Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012
Amended 2019 — enhanced penalties
▾
Act2019 Amendment

⚖️ Key Provisions

Applies to all children under 18 — both boys and girls but disproportionately protects girls. Section 4: Penetrative sexual assault = minimum 10 years, up to life imprisonment. Section 5/6: Aggravated penetrative assault (by a person in authority, gang) = minimum 20 years to death penalty. Child-friendly courts with special procedures are mandatory. Identity of child victims must never be disclosed.

💬 In Simple Words

Any sexual act with a child under 18 is a serious crime. Even "touching" private parts, showing pornography to a child, or using a child for sexual gratification is punishable. The 2019 amendment added the death penalty for rape of a child under 12. Trials must be completed within 1 year. Any person who suspects child sexual abuse must report it to the police — failing to report is itself an offence under Section 21.

ITPA 1956
Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, 1956
India's main anti-trafficking law
▾
Act

⚖️ Key Provisions

Section 5: Procuring, inducing or taking a person for prostitution = 3–7 years rigorous imprisonment. Section 6: Detaining a person in a brothel = 7 years to life imprisonment. BNS Section 370 (Trafficking): Recruitment, transportation, harbouring for exploitation = 7 years to life imprisonment. Trafficking of a child = 10 years to life.

💬 In Simple Words

No one can force, trick, or sell a woman into prostitution. Anyone who runs a brothel, forces women to work in it, or profits from it commits a serious crime. The government provides rescue, rehabilitation (shelter, counselling, vocational training) and legal aid to trafficking survivors through the Ujjawala scheme. Anti-Human Trafficking Units (AHTU) operate in police stations in every district.

Equality & Employment

Equality & Employment Laws

ERA 1976
Equal Remuneration Act, 1976
Equal pay for equal work
▾

⚖️ Key Provisions

Section 4: Equal remuneration for work of same/similar nature. Section 5: No discrimination in recruitment, promotions, training, transfer, or any employment condition on the basis of sex. Employer must maintain a register of workers showing wages paid to men and women separately.

💬 In Simple Words

Same job = same pay, regardless of gender. This applies to government jobs, private companies, factories, shops, plantations — all employers. The law also prevents employers from advertising "only male candidates" for jobs that women can equally perform.

MBA 2017
Maternity Benefit (Amendment) Act, 2017
26 weeks paid maternity leave
▾

⚖️ Key Provisions

Section 5(3): 26 weeks paid leave for first two children; 12 weeks for third child. Section 5(4): 12 weeks for adoptive mother (from adoption date). Section 11A: Compulsory crèche facility for establishments with 50+ employees; women workers have right to visit crèche 4 times a day. Section 5(5): Work from home option after maternity leave (at employer's discretion).

💬 In Simple Words

Working mothers get 26 weeks (6.5 months) paid leave for having a baby — which is among the longest in Asia. You cannot be fired while on maternity leave. Your job is protected and you return to the same or equivalent position. You can also request to work from home after maternity leave ends. Employers who deny these benefits face criminal prosecution.

FA 1948
Factories Act, 1948 — Women's Provisions
Safety, health, crèche, working hours
▾

⚖️ Key Provisions

Section 19: Separate latrines and urinals for female workers. Section 22(2): Safety measures must account for women's safety specifically. Section 27: Women prohibited from cleaning machinery in motion. Section 48: Crèche for factories with 30+ women workers. Section 66: Women cannot work before 6 am or after 7 pm (unless state permits night shifts with safety protocols).

💬 In Simple Words

Any factory with women workers must have separate, clean toilets for women, a safe work environment, and a crèche if there are 30+ women workers. Women cannot be asked to work night shifts unless the state government has made specific rules allowing it with proper safety — including transport and security. Working during night requires the woman's consent.

CA 2013
Companies Act, 2013 — Woman Director
Mandatory board-level representation
▾

⚖️ Key Provisions

Section 149(1): Listed companies and public companies with paid-up capital ≥₹100 crore or turnover ≥₹300 crore must have at least one woman director. SEBI (Listing Obligations and Disclosure Requirements) Regulations 2015 also require that the top 1,000 listed companies by market capitalization must have at least one independent woman director.

💬 In Simple Words

Large, listed companies must legally have at least one woman on their board. This ensures women have representation in the highest corporate decision-making bodies. A company that does not comply faces a penalty of ₹50,000–₹5 lakh and the company officers can be fined ₹10,000–₹1 lakh.

Family & Personal Laws

Family & Personal Laws

HMA 1955
Hindu Marriage Act, 1955
Marriage, divorce, custody for Hindus
▾

⚖️ Key Provisions

Section 5: Conditions of valid marriage — both parties must be of sound mind, not within prohibited degrees of relationship, bride ≥18, groom ≥21. Section 13: Grounds for divorce available equally to husband and wife — cruelty, desertion (2 years), adultery, conversion, unsoundness of mind, communicable disease, renunciation. Section 13B: Mutual consent divorce — after 1 year of separation. Section 26: Custody of children — welfare of child is paramount consideration.

💬 In Simple Words

A Hindu woman has equal rights to seek divorce on multiple grounds — not just cruelty or adultery, but also if the husband has leprosy, is mentally unsound, has a living spouse (bigamy), or has deserted her for 2 years. In custody disputes, the court decides based on what is best for the child — not automatically the father. Women also have additional grounds for divorce under Section 13(2) including if married before 15 (can repudiate at 18).

HSA 1956
Hindu Succession Act, 1956 (Amended 2005)
Equal inheritance for daughters
▾

⚖️ Key Provisions

Section 6 (2005 Amendment): Daughter of a coparcener becomes a coparcener by birth — same right as a son to ancestral property. Section 14: Property possessed by a Hindu female shall be held by her as full owner — not limited owner. Section 15: Female intestate succession — property passes first to sons, daughters, husband, then others (gender-neutral order).

💬 In Simple Words

A daughter is now legally equal to a son in receiving ancestral property — this applies whether or not her father is alive. A Hindu woman's property belongs entirely to her and she can sell, gift, or will it as she pleases. The Supreme Court in Vineeta Sharma (2020) confirmed this right applies retroactively — even if the father died before 2005.

SMA 1954
Special Marriage Act, 1954
Interfaith / civil marriages
▾

⚖️ Key Provisions

Provides for civil marriage between any two persons regardless of religion or caste. Minimum age: bride 18, groom 21. Both parties must give consent freely. Notice of intended marriage must be given 30 days before registration. Section 27: Divorce available on same grounds as Hindu Marriage Act. Applies to all citizens of India and Indian nationals abroad.

💬 In Simple Words

Any woman can marry any man regardless of religion, caste, or nationality through a civil marriage under this Act. No religious ceremony is needed. The marriage is registered with the Marriage Registrar. This protects women choosing inter-faith or inter-caste marriages — their marriage has full legal standing and all rights (maintenance, inheritance, divorce) follow.

MLA 1986
Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986 · Amended 2019
Triple talaq criminalised in 2019
▾
Act2019 Amendment

⚖️ Legal Text

The Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019 criminalizes instant triple talaq (pronouncing talaq three times orally, in writing, or electronically). Declaring triple talaq is void and illegal. Penalty: up to 3 years imprisonment + fine. Additionally, a Muslim divorced woman is entitled to reasonable and fair maintenance beyond the iddat period under the 1986 Act (as interpreted in the Shah Bano case).

💬 In Simple Words

A Muslim husband cannot divorce his wife by saying "talaq" three times in one sitting — this was declared illegal in 2019. Any such pronouncement is now void (has no legal effect) and the husband can be imprisoned up to 3 years. A Muslim divorced woman has the right to maintenance and support — this was established by the Supreme Court in the landmark Shah Bano case (1985) and reinforced since.

BNS 2023 — New Criminal Laws

Criminal Laws Protecting Women

As of July 1, 2024, the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023 replaced the Indian Penal Code. Here are the key sections protecting women.

New Law, Same Protections — Enhanced

The BNS 2023 retained and strengthened most IPC provisions for women, added new offences (organized crime, terrorism-linked crimes), and introduced the concept of community service for minor offences. All major crimes against women remain cognizable and non-bailable.

BNS §63
Rape
Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 · Section 63
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

A man is said to commit rape who has sexual intercourse with a woman under seven circumstances including against her will, without her consent, with consent obtained by putting her in fear of death or hurt, with consent when she believes him to be her husband. Consent means unequivocal voluntary agreement when the woman communicates willingness to participate in the specific sexual act. Absence of physical resistance is not consent.

💬 In Simple Words

Rape is any sexual intercourse without clear, voluntary consent. Silence, being unconscious, or being drunk does NOT mean consent. Punishment: Minimum 10 years rigorous imprisonment to life imprisonment + fine. If the survivor is under 16: minimum 20 years. "Zero FIR" — you can report rape at any police station, not just the local one.

⚠️

PunishmentRigorous imprisonment not less than 10 years to life + fine. Survivor's identity protected — disclosure punishable under Section 72, BNS.

BNS §75
Sexual Harassment
Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 · Section 75
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

A man committing any of the following acts: (i) physical contact and advances involving unwelcome and explicit sexual overtures; or (ii) a demand or request for sexual favours; or (iii) showing pornography against the will of a woman; or (iv) making sexually coloured remarks — commits the offence of sexual harassment.

💬 In Simple Words

Sexual harassment is a criminal offence — not just a workplace complaint matter. Comments about a woman's body, sending inappropriate messages, showing explicit videos without consent, or repeatedly asking for dates after being refused — all constitute sexual harassment under BNS. Punishment: Up to 3 years imprisonment + fine for severe forms; up to 1 year for verbal harassment.

BNS §77
Stalking
Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 · Section 77
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

Any man who follows a woman and contacts, or attempts to contact, such woman to foster personal interaction repeatedly, despite a clear indication of disinterest by such woman, or monitors the use by a woman of the internet, email or any other form of electronic communication, commits the offence of stalking.

💬 In Simple Words

Following someone, repeatedly calling/texting, showing up at their home/workplace, or monitoring their social media/emails after being clearly told "no" — all constitute stalking. Even online monitoring and cyber-stalking is covered. First offence: 3 years imprisonment. Second offence: 5 years imprisonment. Stalking is a non-bailable offence on second conviction.

BNS §79
Voyeurism
Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 · Section 79
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

Whoever watches, or captures the image of, a woman engaging in a private act in circumstances where she would usually have the expectation of not being observed — commits voyeurism. "Private act" includes an act of watching carried out in a bedroom, toilet, bathroom, changing room or any other place which provides seclusion.

💬 In Simple Words

Recording, photographing, or watching a woman in a private setting (bathroom, bedroom, changing room) without consent is a crime. This includes hidden cameras. It also covers sharing such images online. First conviction: 1–3 years imprisonment. Subsequent conviction: 3–7 years imprisonment. If images are shared online (Section 67A, IT Act also applies) punishment is cumulative.

BNS §85
Cruelty by Husband / Relatives (Dowry Harassment)
Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 · Section 85 (formerly IPC 498A)
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

Section 85: "Cruelty" means (a) any wilful conduct likely to drive the woman to suicide or cause grave injury or danger to her life, limb or health; or (b) harassment with a view to coercing her or any of her relatives to meet any unlawful demand for any property or valuable security. This is a cognizable and non-bailable offence — police can arrest without warrant and bail is not automatic.

💬 In Simple Words

If your husband or in-laws mentally torture you, physically abuse you, or harass you for money/dowry, they can be arrested without a warrant. The police cannot refuse to register an FIR. This law covers both physical and psychological cruelty. Punishment: up to 3 years imprisonment + fine. This law is one of the most used women's protection provisions in India.

BNS §124
Acid Attack
Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 · Section 124
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

Whoever throws or administers acid on any person, or attempts to do so, with the intention of causing permanent or partial damage or deformity or burns, disfigures, disables, or maims any person, shall be punished with imprisonment not less than 10 years but which may extend to life, and fine. Acid sales are regulated — sellers must maintain buyer registers.

💬 In Simple Words

Throwing acid on a woman is punishable by minimum 10 years to life imprisonment. Acid attack survivors have specific rights: free first aid and medical treatment at all hospitals (government and private) must be provided immediately without demanding police documents first. The government provides compensation of ₹3 lakh minimum to acid attack survivors under the Nirbhaya Fund.

Part III — Fundamental Rights

Fundamental Rights Protecting Women

These rights are directly enforceable — you can approach the High Court (Article 226) or Supreme Court (Article 32) directly if they are violated.

Art. 14
Right to Equality Before Law
Constitution of India
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

"The State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or the equal protection of the laws within the territory of India." Equality before law = no one is above the law. Equal protection of laws = similar treatment in similar circumstances. Applies to all persons — citizens and non-citizens alike.

💬 In Simple Words

No woman can be given fewer rights or lesser treatment by the government simply because she is a woman. The law applies equally to all. If a law or government action treats women worse than men without a valid reason, it can be challenged in court under Article 14. This article has been used to strike down discriminatory service rules, retirement ages, and exclusions from institutions.

Art. 15
Prohibition of Discrimination on Grounds of Sex
Constitution of India · Art. 15(1) + 15(3)
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

Art. 15(1): "The State shall not discriminate against any citizen on grounds only of religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth or any of them." Art. 15(3): "Nothing in this article shall prevent the State from making any special provision for women and children." This dual structure prohibits discrimination while permitting affirmative action for women.

💬 In Simple Words

The government cannot discriminate against women. But it CAN create special benefits only for women — like women-only colleges, lower exam fees for women, reserved seats in buses, or special scholarships. This is the constitutional basis for all government schemes specifically targeting women. The word "sex" in Article 15 has been interpreted to include "gender identity" by the Supreme Court in NALSA v. Union of India (2014).

Art. 16
Equality of Opportunity in Public Employment
Constitution of India
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

Art. 16(1): "There shall be equality of opportunity for all citizens in matters relating to employment or appointment to any office under the State." Art. 16(2): No citizen shall be ineligible for or discriminated against in respect of employment on grounds of sex.

💬 In Simple Words

Any government job — from IAS to clerk — must be equally open to women. The government cannot have a rule that only men can apply for a post unless there is a very specific functional reason. Women can serve in the armed forces, police, judiciary, and all public service positions. Arbitrary age limits or physical criteria that exclude women without justification violate Article 16.

Art. 19
Freedom of Speech, Movement & Profession
Constitution of India
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

Art. 19(1)(a): Freedom of speech and expression. (b) Right to peaceful assembly. (c) Right to form associations. (d) Right to move freely throughout the territory of India. (e) Right to reside in any part of India. (g) Right to practise any profession, occupation, trade or business. All these rights apply equally to women.

💬 In Simple Words

A woman has the full right to express herself, choose any career, live anywhere in India, and move freely without anyone's permission. These rights cannot be taken away by a husband, family, or community. The Supreme Court has held that "honour killings" and "khap panchayat" diktats that restrict women's freedom of choice in marriage directly violate Article 19.

Art. 21
Right to Life, Dignity & Personal Liberty
Constitution of India — Most Expanded Article
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

"No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law." The Supreme Court has progressively expanded Art. 21 to include: right to live with dignity, right to privacy, right to health, right to education, right to livelihood, right to reproductive freedom, right to clean environment, right to legal aid.

💬 In Simple Words

Article 21 is the most powerful article for women — it has been interpreted broadly by the Supreme Court to include the right to live with dignity (meaning domestic abuse violates it), the right to privacy (reproductive choices are personal), the right to health, and even the right to choose one's partner. Many landmark women's rights judgements — Shah Bano, Vishaka, Puttaswamy — are rooted in Article 21.

Part IV — Directive Principles of State Policy

Directive Principles for Women's Welfare

These are guiding principles for the state — not directly enforceable in court, but courts use them to interpret laws, and Parliament must enact laws consistent with them.

What Are Directive Principles?

Directive Principles (Part IV, Articles 36–51) are like a blueprint for the state. They cannot be directly enforced in court like Fundamental Rights, but they guide legislation and policy. The Supreme Court often reads Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles together.

Art. 39(a)
Equal Right to Adequate Livelihood
Directive Principle
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

"The State shall, in particular, direct its policy towards securing — (a) that the citizens, men and women equally, have the right to an adequate means of livelihood."

💬 In Simple Words

The government must ensure that women have equal access to livelihoods — jobs, businesses, and economic opportunities. This is the constitutional basis for women's self-help group schemes, MUDRA loans for women, and employment guarantee schemes that must include women.

Art. 39(d)
Equal Pay for Equal Work
Directive Principle — basis for Equal Remuneration Act
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

"The State shall, in particular, direct its policy towards securing — (d) that there is equal pay for equal work for both men and women." This Directive Principle was given legislative effect through the Equal Remuneration Act, 1976.

💬 In Simple Words

The Constitution itself directs the government to ensure women get the same pay as men for the same work. The Equal Remuneration Act makes this legally enforceable. Courts regularly use this Article to interpret employment disputes in women's favour.

Art. 42
Just & Humane Conditions of Work + Maternity Relief
Directive Principle
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

"The State shall make provision for securing just and humane conditions of work and for maternity relief." The words "maternity relief" specifically recognize women's reproductive role in the Constitution itself. This is the constitutional basis for the Maternity Benefit Act, 1961.

💬 In Simple Words

The Constitution directly mandates that the state create laws for maternity protection. This means maternity leave, crèche facilities, and protection against pregnancy-based discrimination are not just policy choices — they flow from a constitutional obligation. Companies cannot legally treat maternity as a reason to fire or demote a woman.

Art. 46
Educational and Economic Interests of Weaker Sections
Directive Principle
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

"The State shall promote with special care the educational and economic interests of the weaker sections of the people, and in particular of the Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, and shall protect them from social injustice and all forms of exploitation."

💬 In Simple Words

Women — especially from SC/ST and poor backgrounds — deserve special government support in education and economic opportunities. This is the basis for Kasturba Gandhi Balika Vidyalayas, girl-child scholarships, women's reservation in government jobs, and schemes like Beti Bachao Beti Padhao and Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana.

Art. 51A(e)
Fundamental Duty — Renounce Practices Derogatory to Women
Fundamental Duties · Added by 42nd Amendment, 1976
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

"It shall be the duty of every citizen of India — (e) to promote harmony and the spirit of common brotherhood amongst all the people of India transcending religious, linguistic and regional or sectional diversities; to renounce practices derogatory to the dignity of women."

💬 In Simple Words

Every Indian citizen — not just the government — has a constitutional duty to stop practices that insult or demean women. This includes practices like female foeticide, child marriage, dowry, sati, devadasi system, and sexual objectification of women. While this duty cannot be enforced directly in court, courts use it to justify upholding laws that restrict such practices.

Governance & Political Representation

Women in Governance

Art. 243D
Reservation in Panchayats — 73rd Amendment
73rd Constitutional Amendment, 1992
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

Art. 243D(3): Not less than one-third of the total number of seats reserved for women in Panchayati Raj institutions. Art. 243D(4): Not less than one-third of the offices of Chairpersons at each level reserved for women. Several states increased this to 50% (Bihar, MP, Uttarakhand, Rajasthan, Himachal Pradesh, Odisha).

💬 In Simple Words

At least 33% — and in many states 50% — of elected seats and leadership positions in village panchayats, panchayat samitis, and zila parishads are reserved for women. This has created over 1.4 million women elected representatives in local governance — making India one of the largest experimenters with women's political reservation globally.

Art. 243T
Reservation in Municipalities — 74th Amendment
74th Constitutional Amendment, 1992
▾

⚖️ Legal Text

Art. 243T(3): Not less than one-third of the total number of seats in every Municipality reserved for women. Applies to Municipal Corporations, Municipal Councils, and Nagar Panchayats. Many states have also reserved 50% of mayoral/chairperson posts for women.

💬 In Simple Words

Same as Panchayat reservation, but for urban local bodies — municipal corporations, municipalities, and town councils. At least 1 in 3 elected councillors in any city's municipal body must be a woman. The Mayor or Chairperson position is also reserved in proportion. This gives women a direct voice in city planning, water, sanitation, and urban development decisions.

106th Amdt.
Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam — 33% Reservation in Parliament
106th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2023
▾
2023 Amendment

⚖️ Legal Text

Inserts Articles 330A and 332A — reserving not less than one-third of the total number of seats for women in the Lok Sabha, the Legislative Assemblies of every State, and the Legislative Assembly of the NCT of Delhi. This reservation will come into effect after the next delimitation exercise following the first census after the commencement of this Amendment.

💬 In Simple Words

The Parliament passed a historic amendment in September 2023 that will reserve 33% of Lok Sabha and state assembly seats for women. This is not yet in force — it will be activated after the next census (expected 2026) and subsequent delimitation of constituencies. Once active, this will ensure at least 181 of 543 Lok Sabha seats are reserved for women candidates.
